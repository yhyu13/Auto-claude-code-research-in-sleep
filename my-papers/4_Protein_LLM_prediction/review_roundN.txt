# Critical Review: Protein Language Models for Adaptive Immunity Prediction

## 1. Overall Score and Verdict

**Score: 5/10**

**Verdict: Borderline (Major revisions required)**

The paper provides a timely and broad overview of an emerging field but suffers from significant factual errors, citation problems, unsupported claims, and structural deficiencies that substantially undermine its credibility as an academic survey. While the scope is appropriate and the general narrative is sound, the multiple errors detailed below preclude acceptance in its current form.

---

## 2. Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses

### Strengths

1. **Appropriate scope and organization**: The paper clearly delineates four interconnected domains (MHC binding, TCR specificity, B-cell epitopes/antibody engineering, integrated frameworks) and follows a logical progression from foundations to applications.
2. **Recognition of key limitations**: The authors explicitly acknowledge that no pLLM has demonstrated prospective clinical validation, that class II prediction remains challenging, and that population bias is a significant issue. This intellectual honesty is commendable.
3. **Useful tables**: Tables 1, 2, and 3 provide concise summaries of general pLLMs, TCR models, and antibody models, respectively.
4. **Attention to hybrid approaches**: The discussion of physics-based integration and the positioning of pLLMs relative to other methods (Section 8.6) provides valuable context.

### Weaknesses

1. **Factual errors**: Incorrect CDR3 length ranges, false claim about HLA-A*02:01 prevalence, mischaracterization of AbLang citation.
2. **Citation problems**: Non-existent journal (Nature Reviews Bioengineering), incomplete citations, missing in-text citations, duplicate reference numbers.
3. **Unsupported claims**: Assertions about population allele frequencies, HLA bias effects, and model comparisons lack citations.
4. **Methodological opacity**: Non-PRISMA search without comprehensive coverage of foundational methods.
5. **Comparison fairness issues**: pLLM benchmarks compared against tools that themselves incorporate mass spectrometry data, creating misleading performance impressions.
6. **Structural issues**: Missing citations, inconsistent table formatting, references with missing journal names.

### 3.4 Missing Context

1. **Pre-2019 foundational methods**: The survey begins in 2019 but omits important pre-deep learning methods (e.g., SMM, SMMalign, ARB, ComblIB) that established baseline performance for MHC prediction. These are mentioned briefly in Section 2.3 but not adequately cited.

2. **NetMHC-4.0 and NetMHCpan-3.0**: The historical evolution from NetMHC to NetMHCpan is not adequately traced. NetMHCpan-4.1 is presented as the state of the art without explaining the incremental improvements from earlier versions.

3. **AlphaFold3**: Reference [47] cites AlphaFold 3, but the paper does not discuss its implications for immunogenicity prediction or pLLM competition. AlphaFold 3 now handles antibody-antigen complexes, which is directly relevant to Section 6.

4. **IEDB resources beyond binding prediction**: The IEDB Analysis Resource (Section 2.3) offers tools beyond MHC binding, including T-cell epitope prediction and population coverage analysis, which are relevant to Section 7.3 but not discussed.

5. **Regulatory guidance updates**: Section 8.4 cites FDA guidance from 2014 and 2019 but ignores the 2023 FDA guidance on AI/ML in drug development, which is directly relevant to computational immunogenicity prediction.

6. **Clinical immunogenicity risk stratification**: The FDA's risk-based framework (low, moderate, high risk based on product and patient factors) is not discussed, making the "roadmap to clinical translation" in Section 9 less actionable.

### 3.5 Structural Issues

1. **Inconsistent table formatting**: Table 2 (TCR models) has a truncated final entry. Table 3 (antibody models) lacks complete citations for AbLang.

2. **Reference numbering gaps**: References cited in text do not always match the numbered list (e.g., TCRdist). Some references are cited multiple times with different numbers (e.g., [40] cited correctly, but is [44] a duplicate?).

3. **Section 7.2 missing citations**: The deimmunization multi-objective optimization claim requires citation.

4. **Non-standard literature search**: The paper admits the search "is described here for transparency rather than reproducibility." This is acceptable for a review but means the coverage may be incomplete. Key omissions (noted above) support this concern.

## 4. Recommendations for Revision

### Critical (must fix before acceptance)

1. **Correct CDR3 length ranges** (Section 2.1): Use IMGT canonical ranges—TRA CDR3: 8-25 aa; TRB CDR3: 8-16 aa. Cite Lefranc [61] or IMGT database.

2. **Correct HLA-A*02:01 prevalence claim** (Section 7.3): HLA-A*02:01 is common in many Asian populations. Revise to accurate global frequency data with citation (e.g., IPD-IMGT/HLA database, the Allele Frequency Net Database).

3. **Fix reference [35]**: Verify the correct journal name. If Nature Reviews Bioengineering does not exist, replace with appropriate citation.

4. **Complete all citations**: 
   - Ref [21]: Add full author list and title
   - Ref [22]: Add conference details
   - TCRdist: Add correct reference (likely Dash et al. 2017, Nature 547)
   - Section 7.2: Add citation for deimmunization frameworks
   - Section 7.3: Add citation for HLA allele frequency variation

5. **Fix Table 2**: Complete the NetTCR-2.0 entry.

### Important (strongly recommended)

6. **Fair comparison in Section 4.1**: Clarify that Valsamo et al. fine-tunes ESM embeddings on labeled MHC binding data, making the comparison with NetMHCpan-4.1 somewhat unfair. Discuss whether the AUC improvements reflect model superiority or fine-tuning advantage.

7. **Qualify "atomic-level" claim** (Section 3.1): State that ESM-2 embeddings encode residue-level structural information consistent with, but not equivalent to, atomic coordinates.

8. **Add discussion of AlphaFold3** (Section 6.3): AlphaFold 3 handles antibody-antigen complex prediction, which is directly relevant to CDR-H3 modeling and antibody engineering.

9. **Update regulatory discussion** (Section 8.4): Include the 2023 FDA guidance on AI/ML in drug development.

10. **Provide systematic evidence for success rates** (Section 6.2): Either cite peer-reviewed data or remove the <1-5% estimate. If retained, clarify it is anecdotal from industrial sources.

### Moderate (would strengthen the paper)

11. **Include pre-2019 methods**: Add brief discussion of SMM, ARB, and other pre-deep-learning MHC predictors to contextualize the pLLM revolution.

12. **Clarify AbLang status**: Verify whether AbLang has been peer-reviewed or remains a preprint. If preprint, indicate this clearly throughout the text.

13. **Add quantitative performance comparisons with caveats**: Tables comparing pLLM performance should note that evaluation protocols differ across studies, limiting direct comparison.

14. **Expand population diversity discussion**: Include specific examples of HLA alleles underrepresented in training data (e.g., HLA-B*35:02, HLA-DRB3/4/5 alleles) and their clinical relevance.

15. **Standardize preprint notation**: All preprints should be clearly labeled (bioRxiv/arXiv with DOI) and distinguished from peer-reviewed work in the narrative.

## Summary Assessment

The paper addresses a timely and important topic with generally sound organizational structure and appropriate acknowledgment of limitations. However, the factual errors (particularly the HLA prevalence claim and CDR3 length ranges), citation problems (non-existent journal, incomplete references, missing citations), and comparison fairness issues (misleading MHC benchmark comparisons) constitute fundamental problems that must be addressed. A revised version with corrected facts, complete citations, and more nuanced performance comparisons would be a valuable contribution to the field.
