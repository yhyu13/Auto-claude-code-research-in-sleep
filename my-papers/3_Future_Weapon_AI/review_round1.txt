## 2. ANALYTICAL RIGOR

### Rating: **Weak — Claims Frequently Lack Sufficient Evidence**

#### A. China's Military AI Spending: A Figure Without a Home

The paper claims: "China spends 1.6-2 billion dollars annually on military AI."

This is a critical factual claim about one of the three major actors in the paper's analysis, yet it is cited to a CSET report that itself acknowledges significant uncertainty in these estimates. CSET's own work shows varying estimates from $400-700 million to $1.3-1.8 billion depending on methodology. The authors have apparently cherry-picked the upper bound and presented it as a firm figure.

**Problem:** No credible open-source methodology can produce a precise estimate of China's military AI spending with confidence. The authors should acknowledge this uncertainty explicitly, discuss the range of estimates and their methodological basis, and avoid implying precision where none exists.

#### B. Internal Contradiction on Budget Figures

The paper states:
- "The FY2026 defense budget, exceeding 1 trillion dollars..." (Section 3.1)
- "The FY2025 budget requested 1.8 billion dollars for the second tranche of Replicator..." (Section 3.1)
- "The FY2024 defense budget request included $1.8 billion for the second tranche of its Replicator Initiative" — wait, this text doesn't appear, but the abstract mentions "1.8 billion dollars for the second tranche" and references FY2024 elsewhere.

I cannot verify the internal consistency of these figures because the manuscript has placeholder text and unclear citations. **The authors must ensure all budget figures are internally consistent and traceable to official budget documents, not market reports.**

#### C. The Valkyrie Cost Figure Is Misleading

The paper states: "The Air Force Research Laboratory's XQ-58A Valkyrie, valued at over 3 million dollars per unit..."

This figure appears to be from early program phases. Current estimates for low-rate production quantities of loyal wingman-class systems are significantly higher. More importantly, the per-unit cost figure without context is misleading. The $3 million figure is sometimes cited as the target unit cost for attritable systems, but actual program costs—including development, integration, testing, and support infrastructure—are orders of magnitude higher. The authors should clarify what this figure represents.

#### D. The Kargu-2 Libya Incident Is Contested

The paper treats the 2020 Kargu-2 incident in Libya as an established fact: "a Turkish-made Kargu-2 drone reportedly hunted down and attacked a human target in Libya without a human operator authorizing the specific strike."

**Assessment:** The UN Panel of Experts on Libya reported this incident, and Paul Scharre's *Army of None* (cited here) discusses it. However, Scharre is careful to note the evidentiary limitations: the Panel's report states that the Kargu-2 "was programmed to attack targets" but does not definitively establish that the attack occurred autonomously without human authorization. The Turkish manufacturer STM denies autonomous operation. The authors should hedge this claim and clarify that the incident remains contested.

## 3. BALANCE AND FAIRNESS

### Rating: **Partially Adequate — Improvement Needed**

#### A. Sinophobia Concerns

The paper generally avoids inflammatory Sinophobia. However, certain framing choices warrant attention:

All major powers have reasons for resisting binding constraints—the paper should analyze China's position more sympathetically (not uncritically) to avoid appearing to assign special bad faith to Beijing.

**Specific concern:** The statement "Beijing formally supports a binding LAWS treaty when conditions are ripe" is presented without sufficient analysis of what "when conditions are ripe" means in practice. The paper should explain why analysts view this formulation as a stalling tactic.

## 5. MISSING PERSPECTIVES AND MAJOR GAPS

### Rating: **Significant Gaps**

#### A. No Discussion of European or Other States' Programs

The paper focuses exclusively on the U.S., China, Russia, and Israel. The EU, UK, France, Germany, South Korea, and Turkey all have significant autonomous weapons programs and active positions in the governance debate. Their absence is a major gap in a paper claiming to survey the global landscape.

#### B. Technical Dimensions of the Accountability Gap Are Underexplored

The paper raises the accountability problem but does not seriously engage with:
- How human control can be operationally defined and verified
- What technical mechanisms could ensure meaningful human oversight
- The "responsibility gap" debate in philosophy of AI ethics (which the authors gesture toward but do not develop)
- Existing proposals for "meaningful human control" (e.g., the Montreux Document, the ICRC's two-tiered approach)

#### C. Crisis Stability and Escalation Dynamics Are Underdeveloped

The paper states that autonomous systems "reduce the friction and deliberation time that historically provided opportunities for de-escalation" but does not model how. Specific scenarios that could increase crisis instability should be developed:
- What happens when autonomous systems from two nuclear powers encounter each other in a crisis?
- How do autonomous ISR and strike systems interact in a Taiwan Strait scenario?
- What are the implications of autonomous systems for nuclear signaling and reassurance?

#### D. No Discussion of Offense-Defense Dynamics

The paper does not address whether autonomous weapons are inherently offensive or whether they could favor defense. This is a central question in strategic studies that any comprehensive survey should address.

#### E. Verification and Compliance Challenges Are Ignored

The paper recommends "meaningful human control must be defined operationally" and "binding verification mechanisms," but does not explain how autonomous systems could be verified as complying with human control requirements. Without addressing this, the recommendations are unrealizable.

#### F. The Ethics Section Is Absent

The paper claims to address ethical dimensions (the abstract mentions "the accountability gap") but does not include a substantive section on ethical analysis. What are the moral arguments against LAWS? How do they interact with international humanitarian law principles? What do different philosophical traditions (consequentialist, deontological, virtue ethics) say about delegating lethal decisions to machines?
