# Academic Review: Future Weapons and Artificial Intelligence

**Manuscript Classification:** Survey/Analytical Policy Paper
**Recommended Verdict:** Borderline (5/10)

## 1. OVERALL ASSESSMENT AND VERDICT

**Score: 5.5/10 — Borderline**

This paper addresses an important topic at the intersection of technology, security, and international governance, and its authors demonstrate familiarity with the relevant literatures. However, the manuscript falls short of top-tier publication standards in several critical dimensions: source credibility is uneven, China coverage relies excessively on Western institutional sources, the trilemma framing lacks originality and analytical depth, and key claims lack the evidentiary grounding required for high-stakes policy arguments. The paper would benefit substantially from deeper engagement with primary sources, more rigorous treatment of contested claims, and a more balanced presentation of Chinese and Russian positions.

**Bottom Line:** Publishable with significant revisions, but not yet ready for a top-tier interdisciplinary venue.

---

## 2. DIMENSION-BY-DIMENSION CRITICISM

### 2.1 Factual Accuracy and Source Credibility

**Grade: Marginal (5/10)**

The paper exhibits a significant credibility problem arising from its reliance on secondary and tertiary sources for key factual claims. I flag the following specific issues:

**Citation [8] (Harpy specifications):** The claim that the Harpy "dives to destroy [radar sources] without human approval" is presented as established fact, but operational details of export variants and their autonomy configurations remain classified. The citation to "IAI Harpy SEAD / Loitering Munition Specifications, 2023" is not a primary source; IAI marketing materials cannot be treated as authoritative on operational employment details. This claim requires sourcing to independent defense analysis or documented operational assessments.

**Citation [1] (Kargu-2 Libya incident):** The claim that the Kargu-2 "hunted and attacked a human target in Libya in 2020" is contested. The UN Panel of Experts report describes an incident involving Kargu-2 drones but is ambiguous about the autonomy level employed. The ICTJ and other analysts have disputed whether the drones operated autonomously. Presenting this as established fact without acknowledging the contested nature of the evidence is misleading.

**Citation [9] (Harpy exports):** The claim that the Harpy has been exported to "India, South Korea, Turkey, and other nations" lacks a credible primary source. The specific export customer list for the Harpy is not publicly confirmed by defense trade databases (SIPRI, Trade Registers). This claim should be attributed to defense industry reporting or removed.

**Citation [27] (UNGA resolutions):** The specific vote counts (166 votes, 3 opposed; 156 states) should be verified against official UN records. If accurate, direct UN document citations should be provided rather than secondary attribution.

**The Kargu-2 cost figure ($40,000):** This specific figure appears to derive from open-source reporting but is presented without attribution to a specific source. Is this the unit cost cited by STM, the manufacturer? Without a specific citation, this claim cannot be verified.

**Critical Missing Verification:** The paper states the Harpy has been "operational since 1989" but does not cite evidence of actual combat employment versus test/fielding status. Operational since fielding is not the same as operational in combat.

**Recommendation:** Every specific factual claim requires a primary or rigorously verified secondary source. Marketing materials, non-specialist news reporting, and unattributed internet sources cannot support policy-level claims.

---

### 2.2 Analytical Rigor

**Grade: Weak (4/10)**

The paper's analytical core—the "governance trilemma" framing—suffers from two fundamental problems: lack of originality and insufficient development.

**Trilemma originality:** The claim that this paper introduces the trilemma as a novel analytical framework is incorrect. The tension between military effectiveness, legal compliance, and strategic stability as competing priorities in weapons development appears in prior work by scholars including Heather Harrison Dinniss, Paul Scharre, and Mary Wareham. The paper does not cite this prior literature or acknowledge that it is systematizing existing insights rather than introducing a new framework.

**Trilemma development:** The trilemma is asserted rather than demonstrated. The paper claims states "cannot simultaneously achieve" all three objectives, but does not rigorously analyze why this trade-off is inevitable or examine cases where states have managed similar trilemmas (e.g., nuclear weapons and IHL compliance). The analysis is descriptive rather than analytical.

**Strategic stability analysis:** Section 5.2 on escalation dynamics is underdeveloped. The claim that autonomous systems "reduce friction and deliberation time" is correct but oversimplified. The paper does not engage with the counterargument that autonomous systems might *increase* friction by creating ambiguity about whether an attack was authorized, or that human-out-of-the-loop systems operating in clearly defined parameters (e.g., purely defensive anti-missile systems) might actually *enhance* stability by removing human reaction time from time-critical intercept decisions.

**Verification problem:** Section 4.1 raises the verification challenge correctly but offers no substantive analysis. The paper notes that "technical proposals include hardware-based attestation, behavioral monitoring, and third-party code review" but does not explain how these mechanisms would work, what their limitations are, or whether any state has seriously pursued them. This section reads as a placeholder.

**AI reliability claims:** The statement that "academic studies of computer vision systems under adversarial conditions document error rate increases of 20-40 percentage points relative to baseline performance" (citing Brundage et al. 2020) is misleading. Brundage et al. provide a broad review but do not cite specific studies establishing this range for military-relevant conditions. The citation does not support the specific quantitative claim.

**Recommendation:** The paper needs either (a) to demonstrate that the trilemma framework is applied more rigorously than in prior literature, or (b) to reframe its contribution as a synthesis rather than an analytical advance. All quantitative claims require precise citations to specific studies.

### 2.3 Balance and Fairness (China Coverage)

**Grade: Poor (3/10)**

This is the paper's most serious weakness for a publication claiming interdisciplinary or policy relevance. The China section (3.2) reads as a DoD threat assessment rather than balanced academic analysis.

**Source imbalance:** The section relies almost exclusively on U.S. DoD annual reports, CSET (a U.S.-based think tank), and Western academic analysts. There is no engagement with Chinese primary sources (strategic studies journals, PLA Daily, or Chinese-language scholarship), no engagement with scholars from other countries analyzing China's position, and no acknowledgment that U.S. threat assessments carry institutional biases.

**One-dimensional characterization:** China's LAWS position is presented as obstructionist: "Beijing formally supports a binding LAWS treaty when conditions are ripe" is immediately followed by criticism that its "cumulative threshold effectively excludes most existing systems from prohibition." This framing treats the Chinese position as bad faith rather than engaging with the substantive arguments for it. Why does China hold this position? What are the strategic and legal arguments that support requiring all five criteria simultaneously? The paper does not ask these questions.

**Missing perspective:** Chinese legal scholars and international relations specialists have argued that the five-criteria approach reflects legitimate concerns about definitions creep, about the difficulty of distinguishing autonomy levels in practice, and about the risk that prohibition regimes will be applied asymmetrically. The paper does not engage with these arguments. This is not balance—it is advocacy.

**Misrepresentation risk:** The characterization of China's "doctrinal ambition" as "fundamental[ly] different" from the U.S. approach is overstated. Both states pursue autonomous weapons as force multipliers; the framing differs but the strategic logic overlaps substantially. Conflating doctrinal rhetoric with operational reality produces a distorted picture.

**Specific problematic claim:** The statement that "through Military-Civil Fusion, China seeks to leverage commercial AI advances for military applications, though the practical effectiveness of this integration remains debated among analysts" is weasel language. Which analysts? What are their specific arguments? This vague attribution should be replaced with specific citations and engagement with the substance of the debate.

**Recommendation:** The China section should be substantially revised to include: (a) engagement with Chinese primary sources and Chinese-language scholarship; (b) direct engagement with the substantive arguments for China's LAWS position; (c) acknowledgment of uncertainty and alternative interpretations; and (d) comparative analysis that avoids framing China as uniquely problematic.

### 2.4 Clarity of Argument Structure

**Grade: Adequate (6/10)**

The paper is generally well-organized with a logical progression from conceptual taxonomy to empirical survey to governance analysis to policy recommendations. The section headings are clear, and the paper's structure supports reader comprehension.

**Weaknesses:**

- The transition between Section 3 (empirical survey) and Section 4 (governance analysis) is abrupt. The empirical evidence presented in Section 3 does not clearly motivate the governance questions addressed in Section 4.

- Section 5.1 (The Governance Trilemma) interrupts the flow of Section 5, which otherwise addresses strategic stability and accountability. The trilemma belongs either in the conceptual framework (Section 2) or as a standalone section, not embedded within the stability analysis.

- The policy recommendations in Section 6 are reasonable but lack integration with the preceding analysis. The paper does not explicitly link each recommendation back to the specific governance gaps or trilemma tensions identified earlier.

- The table in Section 3.6 is useful but incomplete and contains the same sourcing problems noted above.

**Recommendation:** Restructure to create stronger logical flow. Consider moving the trilemma to Section 2 or making it a standalone analytical contribution. Ensure policy recommendations are explicitly linked to preceding analysis.

---

### 2.5 Missing Perspectives and Major Gaps

**Grade: Weak (4/10)**

The paper neglects several critical perspectives that would be expected in a top-tier interdisciplinary publication:

**Middle powers:** The paper focuses on the U.S., China, Russia, and Israel, with a brief Europe section. India, Brazil, South Africa, and other states with significant positions on LAWS are absent. These "swing states" in the UN negotiations receive no analysis, despite being essential to understanding governance prospects.

**Non-Western ethical traditions:** The paper's ethical analysis relies entirely on Western philosophical frameworks (Kantian ethics, consequentialism). It does not engage with Islamic just war theory, Buddhist perspectives on violence and agency, or other non-Western ethical traditions that may inform how different states and populations understand the delegation of lethal decisions to machines.

**Civilian AI governance crossover:** The paper treats military AI in isolation from the broader AI governance debate. There is no discussion of how military AI development relates to civilian AI safety, how the EU AI Act or U.S. AI executive orders might inform military AI governance, or how the absence of civilian-military AI governance coordination creates regulatory gaps.

**Counterfactual analysis:** The paper implicitly assumes that autonomous weapons are inherently more destabilizing than human-controlled systems, but does not rigorously examine the counterfactual. Would human-controlled alternatives be more stable? Less stable? The analysis lacks comparative framing.

**Domestic political economy:** The paper does not analyze how domestic political economy factors—defense industry interests, Congressional pressure, Silicon Valley defense contracts—shape U.S. policy on autonomous weapons. This is a significant omission for a policy-focused paper.

**Non-state actor section (5.5):** The claim that "a laptop, open-source computer vision libraries, and a commercial drone are sufficient to build a crude autonomous targeting system" is speculative and unsupported. The paper does not engage with the technical literature on the actual requirements for autonomous targeting, which involves more than off-the-shelf components. This claim overstates the proliferation risk.

**Recommendation:** Add sections or extended discussions addressing: (a) middle power positions; (b) non-Western ethical perspectives; (c) civilian-military AI governance linkages; (d) domestic political economy factors; and (e) more rigorous analysis of non-state actor capabilities.

---

### 2.6 Policy Relevance

**Grade: Adequate (6/10)**

The paper correctly identifies autonomous weapons governance as an urgent policy challenge, and its recommendations—prohibition of anti-personnel autonomous weapons, operational definition of meaningful human control, and independent technical verification panel—are reasonable and align with mainstream policy discourse.

**Weaknesses:**

**Feasibility analysis:** The recommendations are presented without adequate feasibility analysis. The paper acknowledges that "each faces significant feasibility constraints" but does not systematically analyze what those constraints are, which constraints are most binding, or what pathways might overcome them. A policy paper for top-tier venues must engage with implementation challenges.

**Verification panel proposal:** The paper proposes an "independent technical panel to develop verification protocols, modeled on the OPCW scientific advisory process" but does not explain why OPCW is an appropriate model, what specific mechanisms the panel would develop, or how it would overcome the verification challenges outlined in Section 4.1. This recommendation is too vague to be actionable.

**Political feasibility:** The paper does not analyze the political feasibility of its proposals. Given that the U.S. and China both refused to sign the REAIM declaration, what leverage mechanism would bring them to a binding treaty? The paper offers no answer.

**Recommendation:** Strengthen the policy section with: (a) systematic feasibility analysis of each recommendation; (b) specific mechanisms rather than vague institutional proposals; (c) engagement with alternative policy pathways; and (d) honest acknowledgment of what the paper can and cannot offer in terms of implementable solutions.

## 4. CONCRETE REVISIONS TO IMPROVE THE PAPER

### Tier 1: Essential Revisions (Required for Resubmission)

1. **Balance the China section:** Add engagement with Chinese primary sources (PLA strategic studies, Chinese defense white papers, Chinese-language scholarship). Present China's LAWS position as a substantive argument to be engaged rather than dismissed. Include Chinese analysts' arguments for the five-criteria approach. Consider commissioning or citing a response from a Chinese scholar.

2. **Rigorize the trilemma framework:** Either demonstrate that the paper's application of the trilemma is analytically distinct from prior literature, or reframe the contribution as synthesis. Provide rigorous case studies showing how the trilemma plays out in specific contexts (e.g., Iron Dome, Harpy, Replicator).

3. **Verify all factual claims:** Replace non-authoritative sources (IAI marketing materials, Drone Strike, Research and Markets) with primary sources or rigorous academic/government sources. Add specific citations for all numerical claims.

4. **Acknowledge contested evidence:** The Kargu-2 Libya claim should be presented as contested rather than established fact. Add a sentence noting the disputed nature of the autonomy level employed.

5. **Add middle power analysis:** Include substantive discussion of India, Brazil, South Africa, and other states that shape LAWS governance outcomes.

### Tier 2: Strongly Recommended Revisions (Would Substantially Improve Quality)

6. **Develop the verification section:** Section 4.1 is a placeholder. Either develop it with specific technical proposals and analysis, or remove it and focus on governance mechanisms that do not require verification.

7. **Engage with counterarguments:** For every major claim (escalation risk, accountability gap, ethical concerns), engage with the strongest counterarguments. The paper currently presents a one-sided case.

8. **Add political economy analysis:** Analyze how domestic factors shape U.S., Chinese, and Russian positions on autonomous weapons governance.

9. **Strengthen policy recommendations:** Provide specific, actionable recommendations with feasibility analysis rather than vague institutional proposals.

10. **Expand the table:** The current table is incomplete. Consider adding columns for combat deployment status, export status, and human control mechanisms.

### Tier 3: Recommended Revisions (Would Further Improve Quality)

11. **Add non-Western ethical perspectives:** Engage with Islamic, Buddhist, or other non-Western traditions' perspectives on autonomous weapons.

12. **Link civilian and military AI governance:** Discuss how civilian AI governance frameworks might inform military AI governance.

13. **Add comparative counterfactual:** Compare autonomous weapons to human-controlled alternatives on stability and accountability dimensions.

14. **Fix citation formatting:** Ensure all citations conform to a consistent academic citation style.

## 5. FINAL SUMMARY

This paper addresses a critically important topic and demonstrates familiarity with the relevant policy and academic literatures. However, it falls short of top-tier publication standards in several dimensions: source credibility is uneven, China coverage is one-sided, the analytical framework lacks originality and rigor, and key claims are inadequately evidenced. The paper would benefit from deeper engagement with primary sources, more balanced treatment of non-Western perspectives, and more rigorous policy analysis.

**Verdict:** Borderline (5.5/10) — Publishable with significant revisions, not ready for top-tier venue in current form.

The authors should not interpret this review as hostility to the paper's project. The topic is important, the structure is sound, and the authors clearly know the literature. But a top-tier interdisciplinary publication demands more: primary source rigor, balanced analysis, original contribution, and actionable policy recommendations. The paper is a good first draft that needs substantial work to reach its potential.
