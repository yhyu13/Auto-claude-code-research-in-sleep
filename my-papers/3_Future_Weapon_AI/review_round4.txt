## 1. Factual Accuracy and Source Credibility

### Critical Deficiencies

**A. The Kargu-2 Libya Incident (Section 1 and 3.3)**

The paper presents the 2020 UN Panel of Experts report on the Kargu-2 as evidence of autonomous engagement, but the authors themselves acknowledge the evidentiary basis "remains contested." Yet they proceed to cite this incident as foundational evidence in the abstract, introduction, and conclusion. This is intellectually dishonest. The UN Panel's findings were disputed by STM (the manufacturer), the evidentiary chain is murky, and independent verification is lacking. If the authors wish to use this case, they must:

1. Acknowledge explicitly that autonomous engagement has *not* been conclusively demonstrated
2. Distinguish between what the UN Panel reported and what it actually verified
3. Cite the manufacturer's denial and the methodological limitations of the Panel's investigation

Presenting a contested anecdote as established fact in the abstract is unacceptable for a publication claiming academic rigor.

**B. China Military AI Spending (Section 3.2)**

The paper states CSET estimates China military AI spending at "$1.6 to 2 billion dollars annually, though these estimates carry significant methodological uncertainty." This is accurate as far as it goes, but the authors then proceed to treat these figures as reliable inputs to their analysis without adequately foregrounding the uncertainty. CSET's methodology involves estimating from open-source procurement announcements, commercial AI company revenues, and defense budget breakdowns—none of which yield precise figures for AI-specific spending. The authors should either:

1. Present a range so wide it becomes analytically useless (e.g., "$0.5-5 billion")
2. Explicitly state that no reliable figure exists and any analysis built on current estimates is speculative
3. Abandon specific spending comparisons and focus on qualitative indicators

**C. The ASN-301 "Derivation" Claim (Section 2.1 and Table)**

The paper states: "Analysts have noted similarities between the Harpy and China's ASN-301 anti-radiation drone, though the precise derivation chain is difficult to verify through open sources." This hedging is appropriate, but then the table lists the ASN-301 as "Human-out-of-the-loop" with a parenthetical "(allegedly derived; unverified)." This is contradictory. If the derivation is unverified, what is the evidentiary basis for claiming it operates at the human-out-of-the-loop level? The ASN-301 may well be a reverse-engineered Harpy, but open sources do not confirm this, and its autonomy level is certainly not confirmed. This requires stronger sourcing or deletion.

**D. Israel's Defense R&D Budget Claim (Section 3.4)**

"Approximately 15 percent of Israel defense R&D budget, roughly 600 million dollars, focused on AI and robotics [25]." Citation [25] is SIPRI's "Trends in International Arms Transfers and Military R&D, 2024." This is an odd citation for a specific budget claim. SIPRI tracks international arms transfers, not national R&D budgets. The authors should verify this figure against Israeli Ministry of Defense budget documents or the Israel Innovation Authority reports. If the sourcing cannot be confirmed, this specific figure should be removed.

**E. The "Fire-and-Forget" Characterization of Harpy (Section 3.4)**

The Harpy is described as "fire-and-forget" and capable of destroying targets "without human approval." This overstates the case. The Harpy operates within a designated strike zone against specific radar signatures programmed before launch. While no human approval is required for engagement once launched, the target set is human-defined. This is distinct from a system that autonomously selects targets without prior human authorization. The authors should use more precise language: "fire-and-forget *within pre-defined parameters*" rather than the unqualified "without human approval."

## 2. Analytical Rigor

### A. Superficial US/EU/China Comparison

The paper treats the US, China, and Russia as pursuing "aggressive autonomous weapons programs" while "resisting binding legal constraints." This framing is accurate for Russia but analyticallylazy for the US and China.

The US actively participates in CCW discussions and has proposed guiding principles that include meaningful human control. DoD Directive 3000.09, cited in the paper, establishes policy on autonomous weapon systems—something Russia has not done. Presenting the US as simply resisting constraints ignores years of US engagement with the LAWS question.

China's position is more nuanced than presented. Beijing has stated support for a binding LAWS treaty "when conditions are ripe" and has proposed a definition of unacceptable LAWS. The authors characterize this as a "remarkably narrow line" that "effectively excludes most existing systems"—a characterization that may be accurate, but the authors do not interrogate *why* China might prefer this definition or what conditions would make it "ripe." Is this cynical obstructionism? A genuine concern about verification? A desire to constrain US advantages? The paper does not explore.

**Recommendation:** The governance section (Section 4) requires a more granular analysis of each major power's position, their strategic logic, and the areas of potential convergence.

### B. The "Speed-Comprehension Gap" Is Undercauted

Section 5.1 introduces the "speed-comprehension gap" as a major risk: "machines can process sensor data and make targeting decisions faster than humans can verify them." This is plausible, but the authors do not address the countervailing argument: that autonomous systems can also slow down decision-making by introducing additional verification steps, that human operators can be trained to work with autonomous systems, or that the gap is a design choice rather than an inevitable feature.

The authors note automation bias as a concern (commanders deferring to algorithmic recommendations), but they do not engage with the literature on "automation complacency" or the conditions under which human-machine teaming improves versus degrades decision quality. This one-sided presentation weakens the analytical credibility.

### C. Nuclear Escalation Claims Are Speculative

Section 5.4 states: "A single autonomous drone swarm attacking a ballistic missile early-warning radar could, in theory, trigger a launch-on-warning response before human leaders can intervene."

This is presented as a serious strategic risk, but the authors provide no evidence that:

1. Autonomous drone swarms are capable of penetrating to early-warning radar installations
2. Current nuclear command-and-control systems would interpret such an attack as triggering launch-on-warning protocols
3. Any state has autonomous systems positioned to conduct such an attack
4. The attribution ambiguity from such an attack would not lead to deliberation rather than automatic response

The phrase "in theory" concedes this is speculative. The authors should either (a) provide evidence that this scenario is plausible given current capabilities, or (b) reframe this as a speculative future risk rather than a present danger.

### D. Market Size Figures Lack Critical Analysis

The paper states: "The defense autonomous systems market was valued at 18.5 billion dollars in 2025 and is projected to reach 62.4 billion dollars by 2034 [2]."

Citation [2] is the Congressional Research Service report on LAWS. CRS does not typically produce market valuations. This figure likely comes from industry analysts (e.g., Grand View Research, MarketsandMarkets). The authors should:

1. Name the actual source
2. Acknowledge that market projections by commercial analysts have strong incentives to overestimate demand
3. Note that "autonomous systems market" definitions vary widely and often include systems that do not meet the paper's own definitional thresholds

## 3. Balance and Fairness: The China Problem

This is the paper's most serious weakness. The authors oscillate between two problematic positions: (a) presenting China as an existential threat driving an arms race, and (b) accepting Chinese official sources at face value.

### A. Sourcing Chinese Claims Uncritically

The paper cites "Science of Military Strategy 2020" and the "2019 National Defense White Paper" as evidence of Chinese doctrine. These are official PRC publications. The authors do not:

1. Address the gap between doctrinal writing and actual capability development
2. Note that Chinese military doctrine often serves multiple audiences (domestic political signaling, deterrence messaging, bureaucratic budget justification)
3. Engage with Western assessments of how seriously to take "intelligentized warfare" as a doctrinal concept versus a buzzword

The statement that Xi Jinping "urged the PLA to accelerate intelligentization" at the 19th Party Congress is taken from official party documents. This is not evidence of technological capability or even operational intent—merely political direction. The gap between political direction and military capability in China is substantial and well-documented; ignoring it is a significant analytical error.

### B. The "Military-Civil Fusion" Section Is Thin

The paper states: "Through Military-Civil Fusion, China seeks to leverage commercial AI advances for military applications, though the practical effectiveness of this integration remains debated among analysts [3]."

This is a single sentence acknowledging debate, but the paper proceeds as if MCF is delivering operational capabilities. The literature on MCF is far more contested than this suggests. Some analysts argue MCF is a coherent strategy that has yielded results; others argue it is primarily a corruption mechanism for defense procurement; still others argue the defense and commercial AI ecosystems in China are more separate than Western observers assume. The authors should engage with this debate rather than treating MCF as a black box.

### C. The Five-Criteria Definition Is Characterized as Obstruction

The paper quotes analysts characterizing China's five-criteria definition as "drawing a remarkably narrow line." This is one perspective. The authors do not acknowledge that:

1. All states define prohibited weapons narrowly—nuclear weapons treaties prohibit specific warhead types and delivery systems, not nuclear weapons as a category
2. China's criteria (lethal, autonomous, unstoppable, indiscriminate, capable of autonomous learning) might reflect genuine concerns about definition creep
3. The ICRC's own two-tiered approach, which the paper endorses, also involves definitional specificity

Presenting one analytical characterization as though it were established fact is advocacy, not analysis.

## 4. Clarity of Argument Structure

### Strengths

The paper has a clear structure: taxonomy → landscape → governance → risks → recommendations. The table in Section 3.6 is useful. The distinction between human-in-the-loop, human-on-the-loop, and human-out-of-the-loop is well-presented and will be accessible to non-specialist readers.

### Weaknesses

**A. Repetition.** The Kargu-2 incident appears in the abstract, introduction, and Ukraine section. The Harpy's operational history is mentioned in Section 2.1, the Israel section, and the conclusion. The authors should consolidate these references.

**B. Transitions.** Section 3 (landscape) flows into Section 4 (governance) without a clear analytical bridge. The reader expects a transition that connects the deployment evidence to the governance failure. Instead, the paper simply shifts topics.

**C. The Conclusion Is Assertions, Not Synthesis.** The conclusion restates claims from the body rather than synthesizing them into new insights. For a policy paper, this may be acceptable, but an academic survey should offer analytical takeaways that emerge from the evidence presented.

**D. The Recommendation Section Lacks Feasibility Analysis.** The paper recommends: (a) prohibition of anti-personnel autonomous weapons, (b) meaningful human control definitions, and (c) UN technical panel. None of these proposals addresses the political economy of why they might fail or what intermediate steps would build toward them. For a top-tier venue, the authors should engage with the literature on arms control feasibility and the conditions under which prohibition regimes succeed or fail.

## 5. Missing Perspectives and Major Gaps

### A. No Discussion of AI Reliability Under Combat Conditions

Section 5.3 touches on AI failure modes but does not engage with the substantial literature on:

1. Sensor degradation in contested environments
2. Adversarial ML attacks (beyond a single citation to Brundage et al.)
3. The gap between lab performance and operational performance for computer vision systems

This is a significant omission. If autonomous weapons are unreliable under combat conditions—which the evidence suggests—then the strategic stability risks may be lower than the paper implies, but the civilian harm risks may be higher.

### B. No Engagement with the "Counterautonomy" Literature

The paper treats autonomous weapons as if they operate in a vacuum. There is no discussion of:

1. Electronic warfare countermeasures against autonomous systems
2. Cyber vulnerabilities of autonomous platforms
3. The arms race dynamics between autonomous systems and counter-autonomous systems (e.g., spoofing, jamming, adversarial inputs)

This omission makes the proliferation risk section (5.5) one-sided.

### C. No Discussion of the Commercial AI-Defense Nexus

The paper mentions Project Maven and Shield AI but does not analyze:

1. How commercial AI development cycles differ from defense procurement cycles
2. The dual-use nature of autonomous systems technology
3. The implications of commercial AI capabilities outpacing military AI capabilities

This is a significant gap given that the technology前沿 is increasingly driven by commercial, not defense, research.

### D. Non-State Actor Section Is Thin

Section 5.5 treats non-state actor proliferation as straightforward: "A laptop, open-source computer vision libraries, and a commercial drone are sufficient to build a crude autonomous targeting system." This may be technically true, but:

1. The literature on DIY autonomous weapons is sparse—where is the evidence that non-state actors have actually built such systems?
2. The Kargu-2 example involves a state (Turkey) transferring to a non-state actor (GNA/LNA), not a non-state actor building independently
3. The paper does not discuss the organizational and tactical limitations of autonomous weapons for non-state actors who rely on asymmetric warfare

### E. No Engagement with Existing Legal Scholarship

The paper cites ICRC and UN documents but does not engage with the substantial legal scholarship on LAWS. Key scholars absent from the bibliography:

1. Mary Ellen O'Connell on the illegality of autonomous lethal action
2. Jeff McMahan on moral responsibility for autonomous weapons
3. The extensive debate in the *Journal of International Law* and *International Law Studies* on whether existing IHL applies

## 6. Policy Relevance

### Strengths

The paper is clearly policy-relevant, and the recommendations, while inadequately supported, are actionable. The authors are addressing a genuine gap between technology deployment and governance.

### Weaknesses

**A. The "Window Is Closing" Framing Is Unsubstantiated.** The paper repeatedly asserts that the window for preemptive governance is closing. What evidence supports this? Has something changed since 2023 that makes governance more difficult? If the CCW has been stalled since 2013 and autonomous systems have been deployed since 1989, why is the window closing *now* rather than already closed?

**B. The Recommendations Do Not Follow from the Analysis.** The analysis identifies verification as a fundamental challenge. The recommendations include a UN technical panel to develop verification protocols. This does not resolve the verification problem—it delegates it. The authors should explain how the technical panel's work would overcome the incentives to conceal autonomous capabilities that the paper itself identifies.

**C. No Discussion of Alternative Governance Approaches.** The paper focuses on prohibition treaties. It does not discuss:

1. Export controls on autonomous weapons components
2. Confidence-building measures (CBMs) that do not require verification
3. Norms-based approaches (like the Cluster Munition Convention) that build toward prohibition over time
4. Corporate governance (defense contractors committing to human-in-the-loop standards)

A comprehensive policy section would explore the full menu of options, not just the preferred one.

## 8. Concrete Revisions to Improve the Score

### Immediate Corrections (necessary to avoid rejection)

1. **Rehandle the Kargu-2 Libya incident.** Do not present contested evidence as fact. If the case is used at all, it must be explicitly caveated as unverified and disputed.

2. **Verify all market figures and budget claims.** Replace unsupported citations with primary sources. The Brennan Center citation for the US defense budget is particularly egregious.

3. **Remove or radically hedge the ASN-301 derivation claim.** If you cannot verify it through open sources, delete it.

4. **Fix the Harpy export claim.** "Drone Strike" is not an acceptable source for academic publication. Verify through SIPRI's Arms Transfers Database or IAI's official specifications.

### Substantive Improvements (would move to Accept)

5. **Rewrite the China section.** Engage with the contested nature of Chinese military AI assessments. Do not treat official doctrine as operational capability. Include critical perspectives on Military-Civil Fusion effectiveness. Present China's LAWS definition as one of several legitimate approaches, not simply obstruction.

6. **Add a feasibility section to the recommendations.** Why will the proposed measures succeed where CCW has failed? What are the intermediate steps? What are the failure modes?

7. **Engage with the counterargument literature.** The paper is one-sided on speed-comprehension gap, escalation dynamics, and proliferation risks. A balanced treatment would strengthen credibility.

8. **Add a literature review.** The paper assumes readers are familiar with the field but does not position itself within existing scholarship. A brief review of major debates (ICRC vs. DoD positions; prohibition vs. regulation; strategic stability vs. human rights framings) would improve academic credibility.

9. **Develop the verification section.** The current treatment is descriptive. Engage with the technical literature on hardware attestation, behavioral monitoring, and code review. What are the specific technical challenges? What has been tried in analogous domains (e.g., nuclear weapons, cyber weapons)?

10. **Add non-Western perspectives.** The paper is entirely US/European-centric in its sources. Include Chinese, Russian, Indian, or other national perspectives on LAWS governance, even if these must be accessed through secondary Western sources.

## Final Verdict

**Borderline / Weak Reject**

The paper has genuine value as a landscape survey and would be appropriate for a practitioner-focused publication or a policy think-tank report. However, it does not meet the evidentiary standards required for a top-tier academic venue. The authors must decide whether they are writing an advocacy document (in which case the analytical balance problems are less fatal) or an academic survey (in which case the sourcing and balance problems are disqualifying). If the former, they should submit to a policy journal and revise to reflect the advocacy framing. If the latter, they must undertake a systematic revision of every contested factual claim, engage seriously with counterarguments, and develop a more nuanced treatment of China.
