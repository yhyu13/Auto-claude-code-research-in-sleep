# Academic Review

**Manuscript**: Future Weapons and Artificial Intelligence: Autonomous Warfare, Strategic Stability, and the Governance Crisis

**Reviewer's Assessment**: This is a competent survey paper with a broad scope and timely subject matter. However, it falls short of top-tier publication standards in multiple dimensions—particularly factual precision, analytical balance, and the evidentiary basis for several key claims. The review proceeds by dimension, identifies specific questionable claims, and proposes concrete revisions.

---

## Overall Score and Verdict

**Score: 5.5 / 10**

**Verdict: Borderline / Weak Accept**

**Rationale**: The paper addresses an important topic and covers substantial ground. Its taxonomy of autonomy levels (Section 2) is analytically useful, and the governance analysis (Section 4) is largely sound. However, multiple factual inaccuracies, an overreliance on U.S. government sources for China analysis, several under-caveated claims, and a policy conclusion that exceeds the paper's evidentiary warrant prevent a more favorable assessment. A thorough revision could elevate this to 7.5–8.0; as currently written, it would face rejection at Foreign Affairs or Nature.

---

## Dimension-by-Dimension Critique

### 1. Factual Accuracy and Source Credibility

**Score: 4 / 10 — Significant Issues**

The paper contains multiple factual claims that are either inaccurate, insufficiently sourced, or presented with inappropriate confidence. Several examples:

**a) Budget Claim Confusion**

The paper states: "The FY2026 defense budget request exceeds 1 trillion dollars in total, reflecting a significant increase" (Section 3.1). This conflates fiscal years and misrepresents the budget structure. The FY2026 DoD base budget request is approximately $1.016 trillion, but the paper's phrasing suggests this is a significant change from prior years without providing comparison data. More critically, Reference [12] ("Brennan Center, 2026") is cited for both the Maven Smart System contract value and the overall defense budget, but the Brennan Center does not typically provide budget figures of this nature—the authoritative source is the Congressional Research Service or DoD budget documents. The citation chain is unreliable.

**b) Harpy Export List**

Section 2.1 states the Harpy has been exported to "China, India, South Korea, Turkey, and other nations." Open-source verification of specific Harpy export customers is notoriously difficult due to Israeli export secrecy. The paper acknowledges that the ASN-301 "derivation chain is difficult to verify" (Section 2.1), but then proceeds to list China as a confirmed Harpy customer. This is contradictory and weakens credibility. The table in Section 3.6 categorizes the ASN-301 as "Operational (derived)"—the parenthetical requires stronger sourcing or should be removed.

**c) Operational Deployment Claims**

Section 3.3 states Russia and Ukraine are "testing autonomous systems in active combat." The Lancet and KUB loitering munitions operate in human-on-the-loop mode; calling this "autonomous testing" mischaracterizes their operational use. Ukraine's drone operations are largely human-controlled. The claim that "Ukrainian startups have developed ground robots like Makhno" (Section 3.3) lacks any citation and appears speculative. Reference [24] ("CNA, 2024") is cited but presumably covers Russian UGVs, not Ukrainian ones.

**d) Shield AI Operational Claims**

Section 3.1 claims Shield AI's Hivemind "has accumulated thousands of flight hours in operationally representative environments, including GPS-denied, communications-jammed conditions." This is a promotional claim from the company. Open-source evidence for operational deployment is thin. The paper cites no independent verification.

**e) Secretary-General Quote**

Section 4 cites "UN Secretary-General Guterres called autonomous weapons politically unacceptable and morally repugnant, demanding a binding instrument by 2026." I cannot verify this specific quote from the cited sources ([27] UNGA resolution, [28] Lieber Institute). If the Secretary-General made such a statement, it should be attributed to an official speech or press release, not a UNGA resolution or third-party analysis.

**f) European Governance Developments**

Section 3.5 states: "Germany Federal Constitutional Court ruled in July 2025 that drone deployments from Ramstein Air Base require explicit parliamentary authorization." This is an extraordinary legal development—if accurate. The German Constitutional Court has not issued such a ruling. This claim requires immediate verification and likely removal or substantial revision.

**g) XQ-58A Unit Cost**

Section 3.1 cites "target unit cost of approximately 3 million dollars for attritable production quantities." This figure is widely cited from early program estimates, but cost projections for the CCA program have evolved. The citation ([13] Air and Space Forces, 2022) is outdated for a paper being reviewed in 2025.

### 2. Analytical Rigor: Are Claims Supported by Evidence?

**Score: 5 / 10 — Moderate Concerns**

The paper's central thesis—that autonomous weapons are deployed and proliferating, that governance has failed, and that preemptive regulation is urgent—is reasonable and defensible. However, several analytical moves undermine rigor:

**a) Conflation of AI Decision Support with Autonomous Weapons**

Project Maven is repeatedly discussed in a way that blurs the line between AI-enabled targeting support and autonomous weapons. Section 3.1 states Maven "demonstrated that machine learning could dramatically reduce the time required to identify and strike targets" and that "military leaders have credited Maven with greatly reducing the time it takes to identify and strike targets." This implies Maven is part of the autonomous weapons problem. It is not—it is a human-in-the-loop system. The paper distinguishes these categories in Section 2.2, then undermines that distinction in Section 3.1. The Maven discussion should be reframed explicitly as an example of AI integration that raises automation bias concerns, not autonomous weapons proliferation.

**b) Speed-Comprehension Gap Argument**

Section 5 opens with the claim that "machines can process sensor data and make targeting decisions faster than humans can verify them" and that "commanders may become too willing to defer to algorithmic recommendations." This is a plausible concern grounded in automation bias literature, but the paper cites only a single source ([31] ICRC, 2023) without engaging the empirical literature on automation bias in military contexts. The argument is asserted, not demonstrated.

**c) AI Reliability and Failure Modes**

Section 5.3 presents adversarial vulnerabilities and out-of-distribution failures as major risks. The claim that "AI systems performing at 95 percent accuracy in laboratory settings often degrade to 60-70 percent accuracy in contested electromagnetic environments" is cited to [35] (Brundage et al., 2020), a broad AI governance paper, not an empirical study of military AI reliability. The paper conflates general AI robustness literature with specific claims about battlefield performance.

**d) Policy Recommendations Exceed Evidentiary Basis**

Section 6 proposes three concrete actions: prohibit human-targeting autonomous weapons, define meaningful human control through operational requirements, and establish a UN technical verification panel. These recommendations are reasonable, but the paper does not provide sufficient analysis of why these specific proposals would be feasible or effective. The verification problem (Section 4.1) is discussed at length, yet the paper does not explain how its proposed "hardware attestation, behavioral monitoring, and third-party code review" could overcome the verification challenges it describes. The conclusion reads as advocacy rather than analysis.

---

### 3. Balance and Fairness: China Claims

**Score: 4 / 10 — Significant Concerns**

This is the paper's weakest dimension. The China section (3.2) is heavily dependent on U.S. government sources (DoD's annual China military power report, CSIS-adjacent analyses) and presents China's positions in an unfavorable light without engaging Chinese sources or alternative interpretations.

**a) Source Imbalance**

Of 11 citations in Section 3.2, five are U.S. government sources ([3] DoD 2025, [16] State Council 2019, [17] CSET 2023) and two are from U.S.-based think tanks ([18] Qiao-Franco and Bode, [19] Kania and Costello, CSET). There are no citations to Chinese military journals, Chinese Academy of Sciences publications, or independent Chinese-language source analysis. The Science of Military Strategy 2020 is cited through Mayer ([15]), a secondary source. This creates a severe imbalance: China's doctrine, spending, and strategic intentions are reported exclusively through the lens of U.S. analysts.

**b) China's LAWS Position**

The paper states China "formally supports a binding LAWS treaty when conditions are ripe" and then characterizes China's five-criteria definition as "drawing a remarkably narrow line" that "effectively excludes most existing systems from prohibition." This framing is taken entirely from [19] (Kania and Costello, CSET, 2023), a source that is openly skeptical of China's governance positions. The paper does not:

- Engage with Chinese official statements or diplomatic positions at the CCW or UNGA
- Explain the substantive logic of China's criteria (why might a state argue that only systems meeting all five criteria should be prohibited?)
- Consider whether China's position reflects legitimate concerns about verification or definitions, not merely obstruction

A balanced analysis would acknowledge that China's position shares common ground with other states (e.g., calls for meaningful human control) while identifying specific areas of disagreement.

**c) Military-Civil Fusion and DJI**

The paper states: "Through Military-Civil Fusion, commercial technologies from firms like DJI are adapted for military use." DJI is the world's leading civilian drone manufacturer. The claim that DJI technology is systematically adapted for military use under MCF lacks citation. There have been reports of DJI drones being used in Ukraine (by both sides), but this is not the same as formal military adaptation under MCF policy. The paper conflates commercial use with military integration.

**d) ASN-301 Derivation**

As noted above, the paper acknowledges the derivation chain "is difficult to verify through open sources" but then categorizes the ASN-301 as "derived" in the systems table. This is intellectually dishonest. Either the derivation is verifiable (in which case, cite specific evidence) or it is not (in which case, remove the categorization).

**e) Missing: China's Own AI Governance Discourse**

China has published extensive materials on AI governance, including the 2021 Beijing Artificial Intelligence Governance Initiative and subsequent positions at the UN. These are largely absent. China has expressed concerns about autonomous weapons that parallel some Western critiques (e.g., the importance of human control). The paper presents China as uniformly obstructionist, which is inaccurate.

### 4. Clarity of Argument Structure

**Score: 6 / 10 — Adequate**

The paper's structure is logical and follows a clear progression: taxonomy → landscape → governance → strategic risks → policy. The abstract is strong. The introduction's three illustrative cases (Kargu-2, Replicator, PLA doctrine) effectively frame the scope.

**Concerns:**

- **Section 2.2** (AI Decision Support vs. Lethal Autonomy) is conceptually useful but then inconsistently applied in Section 3.1 (Maven discussion).
- **Section 5** mixes strategic stability risks, AI reliability, ethics, and non-state proliferation without clear separation. The subsections are titled 5.1 (implicitly), 5.2, 5.3, 5.4, 5.5, 5.6—note that 5.1 appears to be missing or unnumbered.
- **Section 5.6** (Ethics) is underdeveloped relative to the paper's scope. A few paragraphs on Kantian ethics and the responsibility gap are insufficient given the paper's policy ambitions.
- **Section 6** (Conclusion) restates the paper's findings but does not synthesize them into a clear, prioritized policy agenda. Why these three recommendations and not others? The paper does not explain.

---

### 5. Missing Perspectives and Major Gaps

**Score: 5 / 10 — Significant Omissions**

The paper omits or underdevelops several important dimensions:

**a) Non-State Actors and Proliferation**

Section 5.5 addresses non-state actors and proliferation, but superficially. The claim that "a laptop, open-source computer vision libraries, and a commercial drone are sufficient to build a crude autonomous targeting system" is speculative and unsupported. No evidence is cited. The paper does not discuss existing non-state autonomous weapons use (beyond the contested Kargu-2 Libya case) or analyze the technical barriers to non-state autonomous targeting.

**b) Regional Powers**

India, Pakistan, Iran, and North Korea receive no substantive analysis despite being significant actors in autonomous weapons development and potential proliferation. India, in particular, has an active loitering munitions program and has participated actively in CCW discussions.

**c) Technical Literature on AI Reliability**

The paper's treatment of AI reliability (Section 5.3) relies on a single broad AI governance paper ([35]). It does not engage with:

- DARPA's own studies on AI reliability degradation
- Academic literature on adversarial examples in military contexts
- Empirical data on current system performance in contested environments

**d) Legal Analysis**

The paper mentions international humanitarian law principles (distinction, proportionality, precaution) but does not analyze how these apply to autonomous systems. This is a major gap given the policy recommendations.

**e) Alternative Governance Mechanisms**

The paper focuses on treaty-based governance but does not consider:

- Norms-building through professional military communities
- Industry self-regulation (e.g., defense contractor commitments)
- Bilateral U.S.-China or U.S.-Russia dialogues
- Regional approaches (e.g., EU regulation)

**f) Economic and Industrial Dimensions**

The paper does not analyze the economic interests driving autonomous weapons development. Defense contractors, tech firms, and states all have vested interests in maintaining strategic advantage. The policy recommendations do not account for these incentive structures.

---

### 6. Policy Relevance

**Score: 6 / 10 — Adequate but Overreaches**

The paper is explicitly oriented toward policy, and its governance analysis (Section 4) is its strongest section. The verification problem discussion is nuanced and well-grounded. However:

- The policy recommendations (Section 6) are stated as imperatives ("Three actions are urgent") without adequate justification for why these specific actions would succeed.
- The verification panel proposal is reasonable, but the paper does not explain how it would overcome the political obstacles it describes.
- The paper does not discuss implementation challenges, stakeholder positions, or sequencing.

## Concrete Revisions to Improve the Score

### Priority 1: Factual Accuracy

1. **Remove or verify all unconfirmed claims.** The German Constitutional Court ruling, the Secretary-General quote, and the Shield AI operational claims are the most egregious. Remove them.

2. **Reconcile budget figures.** Clarify the Replicator funding timeline and dollar amounts. Cross-reference CRS reports ([2]) for all budget claims.

3. **Correct the ASN-301 derivation.** Remove the "derived" categorization from the table unless specific sourcing can be provided. Add explicit caveat.

4. **Verify DJI claim.** Either provide evidence of MCF-driven DJI military adaptation or remove the claim.

### Priority 2: Balance (China Section)

5. **Add Chinese primary sources.** Cite Chinese military doctrine publications, official statements at the CCW, or Chinese-language academic analyses. Even translations of Xinhua or PLA Daily reporting would improve balance.

6. **Charitably represent China's LAWS position.** Acknowledge that China's five-criteria approach shares elements with other states' positions (e.g., emphasis on human control). Explain the substantive logic of requiring all five criteria.

7. **Engage China's AI governance discourse.** China's positions on AI ethics and governance are relevant and should not be reduced to obstructionism.

### Priority 3: Analytical Rigor

8. **Separate AI decision support from autonomous weapons.** Reconcile Section 2.2 with Section 3.1. Project Maven should be discussed as an AI-enabled system raising distinct concerns (automation bias), not as part of the autonomous weapons landscape.

9. **Strengthen the AI reliability section.** Cite specific military AI reliability studies (DARPA, service laboratory reports, academic literature). The 95% → 60-70% claim needs a specific source.

10. **Expand the legal analysis.** Integrate IHL principles (distinction, proportionality, precaution) with the paper's policy recommendations.

### Priority 4: Missing Perspectives

11. **Add regional powers.** Include at least a subsection on India, Pakistan, and Iran.

12. **Consider alternative governance mechanisms.** Discuss norms-building, industry self-regulation, and bilateral approaches.

13. **Engage with counterarguments.** Address the "humans are the problem" argument (human operators make worse decisions than algorithms), the verification feasibility debate, and alternative policy frameworks.

### Priority 5: Policy Recommendations

14. **Ground the recommendations in analysis.** Section 6 should explain why these three recommendations, why now, and what obstacles they face.

15. **Add implementation discussion.** Address sequencing, stakeholder positions, and enforcement mechanisms.

## Final Verdict

**Borderline / Weak Accept**

**Condition for Acceptance**: The paper requires substantial revision before publication. The factual errors must be corrected, the China section must be rebalanced with primary sources, and the policy recommendations must be grounded in the paper's own analysis. If these revisions are made rigorously, the paper could be elevated to Strong Accept. If the authors are unwilling to correct the German Constitutional Court claim and other identified errors, the paper should be rejected.

**Top-Tier Venue Readiness**: The paper's scope and ambition are appropriate for a top-tier venue, but its execution falls short. A Nature or Foreign Affairs review would likely identify the same issues and recommend major revision or rejection. The authors should treat this review as a guide for substantive improvement, not as a gatekeeping exercise.
