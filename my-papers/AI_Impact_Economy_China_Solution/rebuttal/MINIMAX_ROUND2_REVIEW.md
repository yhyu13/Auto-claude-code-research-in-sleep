## II. NEW Issues Introduced by the Revisions

The authors' revision process has introduced problems that were not present in the earlier draft.

### 2.1 Section Numbering Error: Missing Section 3.5

The paper jumps from Section 3.4 (Industrial Policy: The Big Fund and Local Government Competition) directly to Section 3.6 (State Capacity Constraints). **Section 3.5 is absent without explanation.** This is either:

(a) A ghost citation: text that existed in the draft being reviewed was deleted but the section number was not removed from the table of contents or cross-references, or
(b) A drafting artifact: the authors created Section 3.5 during revision but inadvertently deleted it during final assembly

In either case, this is an elementary editorial error that must be corrected before publication. If there is intended content for Section 3.5, it must be restored. If the number was used erroneously, it must be removed.

### 2.2 Unsupported Comparative Claim in the Abstract

The Abstract states:

> "China has pursued one of the world's most aggressive and systematically orchestrated national AI strategies"

The comparative qualifier "one of the world's most aggressive" is stated without evidence. **South Korea's K-Chips Act (2024) represents approximately $19 billion in semiconductor subsidies** against a GDP of roughly $1.7 trillion—a subsidy-to-GDP ratio that arguably exceeds China's program. Japan's Society 5.0 and associated semiconductor initiatives are similarly substantial. The US CHIPS Act commits $52 billion.

If the claim is meant to be ordinal ("among the top tier"), it should specify what dimension (scale, ambition, speed of regulatory deployment) and provide comparative data. If the claim is meant to be absolute ("the most aggressive"), it is demonstrably false for at least South Korea. **This claim should be removed or substantiated.**

### 2.3 Table Formatting Inconsistencies

The comparative table in Section 6 has formatting problems that suggest hasty assembly:

- The "European Union" column contains duplicate entries (two rows for "Speed of regulation" and "Data governance")
- The table structure does not align with the text's discussion, which does not reference the table by number
- The Japan/South Korea columns contain minimal data (mostly empty cells), suggesting the authors intended to expand this but did not

These are correctable editorial issues, but they suggest the table was not subjected to final proofreading.

### 2.4 Probability Estimates Without Methodology

The scenario framework now assigns numerical probabilities (55%, 20%, 15%, 10%) without any stated methodology for how these were derived. In a peer-reviewed paper, readers must be able to evaluate the evidential basis for probability assignments. Are these:

- Derived from structural model estimation?
- Based on expert elicitation?
- Heuristic intuitions of the authors?
- Placeholder values intended to illustrate uncertainty ranges?

**Recommendation:** State explicitly how these probabilities were generated, or replace them with qualitative descriptors ("more likely than not," "low probability," "tail risk") that do not imply spurious precision.

### 2.5 Remaining Weaknesses from the Original Review

#### (a) Private Sector Actors Remain Underdeveloped

The paper still lacks a substantive analysis of how private sector actors (Alibaba, Tencent, ByteDance, DeepSeek, ByteDance's AI initiatives) interact with state coordination. The claim that "the same state apparatus that subsidizes AI development also regulates its deployment" is accurate but incomplete. The political economy of private firm-state relations, including platform rectification (2020–2021) and subsequent rehabilitation of tech champions, deserves analytical attention.

#### (b) Japan/South Korea Comparisons Are Superficial

The comparative table includes columns for Japan and South Korea but provides almost no substantive analysis of their AI strategies. The paper references the K-Chips Act and SIP in passing but does not analyze:

- Why South Korea's semiconductor strategy differs from China's
- How Japan's Society 5.0 public-private partnership model compares to China's state-led approach
- What lessons (if any) these cases offer for the China Solution framework

**The comparison appears to exist for completeness rather than analytical purpose.**

## III. Remaining Deficiencies Not Adequately Addressed

### 3.1 The "Institutional Innovation" Claim Requires Theoretical Grounding

The paper repeatedly characterizes the China Solution as "a genuine institutional innovation in technology governance" (Abstract) and "distinctive institutional innovation" (Section 3). However:

- No definition of "institutional innovation" is provided
- No citation to the institutional economics literature (North, Ostrom, Acemoglu and Robinson, etc.) that would legitimate the category
- No comparison with other cases of institutional innovation in technology governance (e.g., Germany's Fraunhofer model, Taiwan's ITRI, Singapore's EDB)

The claim is suggestive but unsupported. If the authors intend to argue that the China Solution represents a genuinely novel governance model, they must define their terms and provide comparative evidence.

### 3.2 The Claim That "Enforcement Is Operational, Not Merely Aspirational"

Section 3.3 states that enforcement is "operational, not merely aspirational" and cites three enforcement actions (Shanghai CAC penalties, Zhejiang CAC removals). Three enforcement actions over three years is a thin evidentiary base for this strong claim. China's regulatory enforcement is notoriously uneven: rules are often selectively enforced as a governance tool rather than uniformly applied. The paper should nuance this claim or provide more comprehensive enforcement data.

### 3.3 Internal Regional Variation Is Noted But Not Analyzed

Section 7.2 acknowledges that "Beijing, Shanghai, Shenzhen, and Hangzhou concentrate the vast majority of China's AI development" while "interior provinces lag." This is accurate but underspecified. The paper would benefit from:

- A brief analysis of why concentration occurs (talent pooling, VC availability, policy proximity)
- Evidence on whether the AI+ initiative is successfully reducing this gap or exacerbating it
- Implications for labor market analysis (displacement concentrated in coastal manufacturing provinces)

### 3.4 The "New Quality Productive Forces" Concept Is Introduced But Not Analyzed

The paper mentions this concept (Section 3.2) but does not explain its theoretical content. "New quality productive forces" is a Xi Jinping-era economic concept that signals a break with previous growth models (extensive growth, high-speed industrialization). The paper should briefly situate this concept within Chinese economic policy discourse.

## V. Recommendation

**Score: 7.0/10 (Accept with Reservations)**

The paper has improved meaningfully from 5.5/10 to 7.0/10. The authors demonstrably read the review carefully and addressed the majority of specific criticisms. The sourcing improvements alone justify a full point increase. The new material on Taiwan, state capacity constraints, and military-civil fusion is analytically valuable.

However, the paper has not reached the standard of a strong accept for three reasons:

1. **The missing Section 3.5 is an elementary editorial error** that cannot appear in a published paper. It suggests rushed final assembly rather than thorough proofreading.

2. **The "most aggressive" comparative claim is unsupported** and, for South Korea, likely false. It should be removed or qualified.

3. **The private sector analysis remains underdeveloped.** In a paper that claims to analyze "the China Solution," the role of private firms in navigating state coordination is not optional—it is central to understanding whether the model is truly "state-led" or "state-coordinated with significant private agency."

- Provide additional evidence for the enforcement claim or soften the claim
